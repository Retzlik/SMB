## 4. Content Architecture: What Broadly Does Badly That Karl Should Do Better

### 4.1 Templated vertical pages
Broadly's sub-vertical pages are embarrassingly templated. Every single one follows the same structure: "In the past, [INDUSTRY] businesses relied on word of mouth and local advertisements to grow their businesses. Now, more effective, less resource-heavy marketing tools are available..." with the industry name swapped in.

**Karl's opportunity:** Write genuinely different vertical pages. A dental practice page should talk about patient trust, Jameda reviews, and appointment booking. A plumber page should talk about missed calls on job sites and emergency search queries. An accountant page should talk about referral dependency and tax season marketing. Each vertical has genuinely different pain points — Karl's pages should reflect that.

### 4.2 Product-first framing
Broadly frames everything as software features: "Reputation AI", "CRM AI", "Conversations AI". Their pages describe what the software does, not what the business owner experiences.

**Karl's opportunity:** Frame everything as capabilities of your agent. Not "Reputation AI" — instead "Every review answered. New ones every week." Not "Conversations AI" — instead "Never miss a call. Even at midnight." The framing is: what does Karl do for you, not what features does the platform have.

### 4.3 Social proof
Broadly claims "11,600 businesses" and has 3-4 rotating testimonials with specific metrics ("reviews increased from 151 to 239 in two months"). They show integration partner logos (ServiceTitan, Jobber, QuickBooks).

**Karl's opportunity:** At launch, we have zero customers. We cannot fabricate social proof. Instead:
- "Built in Stockholm. GDPR-native." (origin trust)
- Integration logos (Google, WhatsApp, Facebook, Trustpilot — platforms we connect to)
- "What happens when you sign up" (process trust, honest timelines)
- As customers come: real testimonials with specific metrics, following the locked structure

### 4.4 Pricing transparency
Broadly shows prices on the pricing page but hides the $350 onboarding fee in small text. Their à la carte pricing is confusing — you can buy individual AI employees OR bundles, but it's unclear what overlaps.

**Karl's opportunity:** Radically simple. Three tiers. Prices visible everywhere (homepage, pricing page, vertical pages). "No contracts. No onboarding fees. Cancel anytime." This is our strongest substance-disruptor signal.

### 4.5 European gap
Broadly has zero European content. No German pages, no European review platforms (Jameda, Trustpilot, ProvenExpert, Kiyoh), no GDPR messaging, no WhatsApp integration emphasis, no European phone numbers. Their entire site assumes US business owners using SMS.

**Karl's opportunity:** This is the strategic gap. Every page should feel European by default. German directories, European review platforms, WhatsApp as the primary messaging channel, GDPR badges, euro pricing, "Built in Stockholm" trust signal.

### 4.6 AI Workforce vs. Agent
Broadly now positions as "AI Workforce" with multiple AI employees (Receptionist, Reputation Specialist, Content Writer, Sales Assistant). This fragments the experience — the customer has to understand which AI employee does what.

**Karl's opportunity:** One agent. One name. One relationship. Karl does reviews AND listings AND social AND ads AND receptionist AND campaigns. The simplicity of "one agent" vs. "five AI employees" is a genuine differentiator.
